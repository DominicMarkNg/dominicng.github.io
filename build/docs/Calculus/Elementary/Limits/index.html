Limits

In the study of calculus, understanding the concept of a limit is a stepping stone towards delving into continuous functions, derivatives, and integrals. The formal definition, often referred to as the ϵ\epsilonϵ-δ\deltaδ definition, is important yet can initially seem impenetrable. These are my notes on the formal definition of limit aimed towards building an intuitive understanding.

Formal Definition​

The formal definition of limit hinges on the notions of arbitrarily small quantities, encapsulated by Greek letters ϵ\epsilonϵ (epsilon) and δ\deltaδ (delta). Given a function f(x)f(x)f(x), the statement

lim⁡x→af(x)=L\lim_{{x \to a}} f(x) = Lx→alim​f(x)=L

The crux of this definition lies in the quantifiers. It's about guaranteeing that f(x)f(x)f(x) gets arbitrarily close to LLL as xxx approaches aaa, by making δ\deltaδ small enough.

Dissecting the Formalism​

Let's dissect the components of this definition:

  1. The Limit Point (aaa): This is the point that xxx is approaching.
  2. The Limit Value (LLL): This is the value that f(x)f(x)f(x) is approaching as xxx approaches aaa.
  3. The Epsilon (ϵ\epsilonϵ): Represents how close f(x)f(x)f(x) is to LLL.
  4. The Delta (δ\deltaδ): Represents how close xxx is to aaa.

The inequalities 0<∣x−a∣<δ0 < |x-a| < \delta0<∣x−a∣<δ and ∣f(x)−L∣<ϵ|f(x) - L| < \epsilon∣f(x)−L∣<ϵ are crucial as they formalize the notion of "closeness".

Bridging to Intuition​

The ϵ\epsilonϵ-δ\deltaδ definition at first might seem detached from intuition. However, a geometric interpretation can provide a clearer picture.

Geometric Interpretation​

Imagine a graphical representation where the function f(x)f(x)f(x) is plotted against xxx. The point x=ax = ax=a is where we are focusing, and LLL is the value that f(x)f(x)f(x) is approaching. Now, envision two horizontal lines at L+ϵL + \epsilonL+ϵ and L−ϵL - \epsilonL−ϵ. Similarly, draw two vertical lines at a+δa + \deltaa+δ and a−δa - \deltaa−δ. The essence of the definition is that as xxx moves within the vertical bounds, f(x)f(x)f(x) stays within the horizontal bounds.

Concrete Scenario​

Consider a real-world scenario where a car's speedometer approaches a certain speed as the car accelerates. Though the needle might never hit the exact speed due to mechanical limitations, it gets arbitrarily close. This scenario mirrors the behavior of limits, where f(x)f(x)f(x) gets arbitrarily close to LLL as xxx gets arbitrarily close to aaa.

Through this blend of formalism and intuition, the concept of limits becomes less daunting, serving as a solid foundation for further exploration in calculus.
